Katherine Johnson

One of the first African-American women to work as a NASA scientist

Here's a time line of Katherine Johnson's life:

  • 1918 - Born in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia
  • 1928 - Attends high school on the campus of West Virginia State College.
  • 1932 - Graduates high school and enrolls at West Virginia State College, an historically black college, and enrolls in every mathematics course.
  • 1937 - Graduates with honors from West Virginia State College.
  • 1952 - Learns that the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) was hiring mathematicians. NACA was hiring African-American mathematicians as well as whites for their Guidance and Navigation Department.
  • 1953 - Starts working for NACA as a 'computer.'
  • 1958 - Becomes an aerospace technologist with the newly-formed NASA.
  • 1969 - Calculates the trajectory of Apollo 11 flight to the moon.
  • 1970 - Worked on the return trajectory of the aborted Apollo 13 mission, which helped the crew to return safely.
  • 1999 - President Obama presents Katherine with the Presidential Medal of Freedom.
  • 2016 - NASA presents Katherine with a NASA Group Acheivement Award.

"We needed to be assertive as women in those days – assertive and aggressive – and the degree to which we had to be that way depended on where you were. I had to be. In the early days of NASA women were not allowed to put their names on the reports – no woman in my division had had her name on a report. I was working with Ted Skopinski and he wanted to leave and go to Houston ... but Henry Pearson, our supervisor – he was not a fan of women – kept pushing him to finish the report we were working on. Finally, Ted told him, "Katherine should finish the report, she's done most of the work anyway." So Ted left Pearson with no choice; I finished the report and my name went on it, and that was the first time a woman in our division had her name on something."

-- Katherine Johnson
